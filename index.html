How I work.

I start with research — understanding the space before touching a single frame. Coding is not my core skill, but that has never been the point.1 What I carry is a precise sense of the feeling something should produce — how it should resist, respond, and breathe under a finger or a cursor.

I describe that feeling through whatever language closes the gap fastest: a prompt, a diagram, a reference, a margin note. The more precisely I can articulate the intention2, the less I need to hand-write the implementation. That is the trade I have made, and I think it is an honest one.

There is a shift happening that I find genuinely interesting — the distance between a wireframe and a first coded draft is collapsing.3 The canvas is becoming optional. Ideas move straight from thought to prompt to running interface. But the designer's job does not shrink in that translation — it expands. Defining the right problem, setting visual direction, building systems that hold under pressure, making decisions that serve real people in real contexts. That work does not get prompted away.

[1]

The point is knowing what should exist and why. Implementation is a constraint, not the definition of the work.

[2]

References, motion notes, annotated screenshots — anything that makes the vision unambiguous before a line of code is written.

[3]

Figma may not be where ideas are born for much longer. The prompt is the new canvas — but the designer still owns the strategy, the system, and the judgment behind it.
